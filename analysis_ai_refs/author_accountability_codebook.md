# Codebook: Author Accountability Modes in Discursive AI Mentions

Scope: applied only to **author-authored** AI mentions in the
**discursive** subset (i.e., the PR author is the speaker, and the
comment body does *not* `@`-mention an AI bot account). These are
the cases where an author publicly references AI tools in prose
during a PR discussion.

The coding question: *when an author mentions AI in prose, what
accountability work is the mention doing?*

## Primary categories (choose one)

### `provenance_only`
Minimal disclosure of AI involvement with no further claim, defense,
or partition. The mention is a bare attribution or a process
breadcrumb. No evaluative frame around the AI's contribution.

Typical markers:
- "Co-authored with Claude"
- "Generated with Copilot"
- "Based on a ChatGPT draft"
- Single-line provenance with no surrounding justification text

### `defensive_justification`
Author acknowledges AI use but actively frames their own verification,
review, or retention of responsibility. The speech act pushes back
against an implied or explicit suspicion that AI use might be
problematic or lazy.

Typical markers:
- "I used Copilot but verified each line"
- "The AI drafted this; I reviewed thoroughly"
- "Claude generated the skeleton, I adapted it for our style"
- Explicit appeal to human labor *in tension with* AI involvement

### `credit_partition`
Author explicitly distinguishes which parts came from AI and which
parts are their own. Unlike `defensive_justification`, the work is
allocation rather than defense: there is an attempt to give an
accurate accounting of the division of labor, often neutrally.

Typical markers:
- "Copilot generated the boilerplate; I wrote the logic."
- "The test cases are AI-generated, the refactor is mine."
- "AI handled the translation; I did the review."
- Structural separation of AI-contributed vs human-contributed parts

### `fault_to_ai`
Author attributes a bug, error, omission, or limitation to AI
output. Often appears as a commitment to fix, correction already
made, or explanation of why something is wrong.

Typical markers:
- "Copilot missed this edge case"
- "This was AI-generated and hallucinated the API"
- "Claude got the logic backwards; fixed in next commit"
- AI is positioned as the source of a problem the author is now
  addressing

### `process_documentation`
Author describes the *process* of working with AI — the prompt
sequence, the iteration, the back-and-forth — as a way of
establishing transparency or reproducibility. Distinct from
`provenance_only` because the focus is on the *procedure*, not just
the fact of AI involvement.

Typical markers:
- "I ran this through Claude with the following prompt: ..."
- "After three iterations with Copilot, I settled on ..."
- "I fed the spec to GPT, then manually merged the suggestions"
- Process is the evidence

### `other`
AI is mentioned in author prose but the mention does none of the
above. Covers off-topic references, identifier matches (repo names,
URLs containing AI keywords), and mentions where AI is a topic of
discussion but not an object of accountability.

## Decision rules

1. Code the **dominant** accountability move. If a mention does two
   things (e.g., provenance + defense), code the stronger one as
   primary and note the secondary.

2. `provenance_only` vs `defensive_justification`: if there is any
   reviewer-facing reassurance language ("but", "I checked", "I
   verified", "as always"), prefer `defensive_justification`.

3. `credit_partition` vs `defensive_justification`: if the author
   is evenly reporting contributions without defending, use
   `credit_partition`. If the phrasing is asymmetric (human effort
   elevated, AI minimized), use `defensive_justification`.

4. `process_documentation` requires *procedure*, not just output.
   A single "generated by X" is `provenance_only`, not
   `process_documentation`.

5. When in doubt between `other` and a substantive category, prefer
   `other`. The categories should be earned, not assumed.

## What this coding buys for the paper

This coding substantiates the claim that author-authored discursive
AI mentions function as **accountability performance** rather than
mere documentation. The distribution across these five categories
tells us *which accountability register* is dominant among OSS
authors: self-defense, neutral partition, fault-shifting,
procedural transparency, or bare disclosure. Each is a different
move in the same game — public construction of responsibility for
AI-assisted work in front of a reviewing audience.
